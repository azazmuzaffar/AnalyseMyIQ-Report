What is IQ?

At its core, IQ is a measure of your ability to reason. What makes IQ so unique is that it basis itself off of all the previous scores achieved on that particular assessment (with the aim to make 100 the most common score) and variations of an IQ test are used to determine:

  • A A prediction of how well a child will perform academically
  • RThe effectiveness of ADHD treatments
  • QHow effective a potential employee will be

Despite being so widely used, IQ has become a controversial subject with one of the main criticisms being that it isn’t a good predictor of success.

We agree.

How is IQ worked out?

Once you’ve answered the IQ Test questions your results are compared with data from other participants in your age group who have already taken the test and then a normalized score is calculated.

Normalizing means the average IQ score is 100. Your score will show where you stand compared to other peers in your age group who have taken the test. IQ scores follow a normal distribution of statistical data. Approximately 95% of the world’s population have scores within two standard deviations (SD) of the mean (average IQ score), meaning that 95% of the population stand within a range of 70 to 130, and 98% are below 131.

IQ points are not percentage points. While one standard deviation is 15 points, and two SDs are 30 points, and so on, this does not imply that mentalability is linearly related to IQ, such that IQ 50 means half the cognitive ability of IQ 100.

Verbal Intelligence

Verbal Intelligence is the ability to use language to solve problems and analyse information.

How is Verbal Intelligence
used in daily life?

You use your verbal intelligence whenever you engage the language centres of your brain. This can be used for simple tasks like reading signs, to more complex tasks like giving an oral synopsis of a written report.

Those with a high level of Verbal Intelligence are typically found in roles that require the ability to communicate complex ideas, to understand and parse a lot of written material, and to express themselves using language.

Key Stengths

Reading, Writing, Speaking

Non-Verbal Intelligence

Sometimes called “abstract reasoning,” your Non-Verbal Intelligence is engaged when solving problems presented as a diagram or pictorially.

How is Non-Verbal Intelligence
used in daily life?

Your Non-Verbal Intelligence is activated when presented with non-language based visual information (such as a map, or furniture building instructions). It is also used when trying to understand abstract concepts that are subjective such as morality or ethics.

With high Non-Verbal Intelligence, you’re best suited to solving problems that involve using logic or “common sense”. Those that score highly in Non-Verbal Intelligence are also suited to explaining diagrams and integrating those systems into the physical world.

Key Stengths

Pattern Recognition, Understanding Diagrams, Outcome planning

Numerical Intelligence

Numerical Intelligence is the ability to use numbers and data to solve problems and analyse information.

How is Numerical Intelligence

A high Numerical Intelligence is essential for those that want to solve scientific problems or any tasks that require the use of numerical data to prove a solution.

Spatial Intelligence

Spatial Intelligence is the ability to understand physical and mechanical systems and mentally visualise complex 3D constructs.

How is Spatial Intelligence
used in daily life?

When you try and imagine how something will look (such as new clothes or furniture) you’re activating your Spatial Intelligence. Similarly, it’s activated when you’re trying to navigate spaces, remember where you are, and know where you’re going.

Those with high levels of Spatial Intelligence are likely to find themselves solving problems that require them to mentally build models and then create those models in the physical world, such as an Architect or Chemist.

Key Stengths

Navigation, Design, Construction
